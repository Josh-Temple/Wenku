### id: TERM-0001
term: Spacing Effect
domain: Learning Science
status: complete
one_liner_definition: Learning is retained better when study sessions are distributed over time.
clear_definition: The spacing effect is the finding that information is remembered more reliably when practice is spread across intervals instead of massed in a single session.
practical_relevance: It improves long-term retention in exam prep and skill training.
canonical_example: Reviewing vocabulary on day 1, day 3, and day 7 outperforms repeating all reviews on day 1.
common_misuse: Assuming any delay helps learning, even when intervals are too long to maintain retrieval success.
proposer_or_origin: Hermann Ebbinghaus and later memory research.
year_or_period: 19th century origin, modern replications ongoing.
contrast_points: Different from simple repetition because timing, not only frequency, drives retention.
evidence_level: Strong
sources:
  - Cepeda et al. (2006), Psychological Science
confusion_cluster:
  - TERM-0003
  - TERM-0005

### id: TERM-0006
term: Desirable Difficulties
domain: Learning Science
status: complete
one_liner_definition: Challenges in learning that boost long-term retention.
clear_definition: Desirable difficulties refer to learning conditions that introduce challenges, making initial acquisition slower but leading to superior long-term retention, retrieval, and transfer of knowledge.
practical_relevance: It guides instructors to design effortful learning activities that improve durable understanding rather than short-term fluency.
canonical_example: Varying practice conditions, like solving problems in different contexts, instead of repetitive drills.
common_misuse: Assuming any difficulty is desirable; excessive challenges can hinder learning if they overwhelm cognitive capacity.
proposer_or_origin: Robert Bjork
year_or_period: 1994
contrast_points: Different from unproductive struggle because the challenge is strategically introduced to improve encoding without causing persistent failure.
evidence_level: High
sources: []
confusion_cluster:
  - Cognitive load
  - Flow state

### id: TERM-0007
term: Dual Coding
domain: Learning Science
status: complete
one_liner_definition: Combining verbal and visual representations to improve learning.
clear_definition: Dual coding theory posits that information is processed through verbal and visual channels, and combining both (for example, words with images) enhances learning, memory, and comprehension compared to using one modality alone.
practical_relevance: It informs instructional design by encouraging coordinated text-and-visual explanations for stronger comprehension and recall.
canonical_example: Using a labeled diagram alongside a textual explanation to teach human anatomy.
common_misuse: Adding decorative images that distract rather than support the verbal content.
proposer_or_origin: Allan Paivio
year_or_period: 1971
contrast_points: Unlike single-modality learning, dual coding leverages parallel processing channels for richer encoding.
evidence_level: High
sources: []
confusion_cluster:
  - Multimedia learning
  - Visual aids

### id: TERM-0008
term: Elaboration
domain: Learning Science
status: complete
one_liner_definition: Linking new information to prior knowledge to enhance memory.
clear_definition: Elaboration involves enriching new information by connecting it to existing knowledge, such as explaining why or how something works, which deepens understanding and improves long-term memory retention.
practical_relevance: It helps learners build meaningful associations that make retrieval and transfer more reliable.
canonical_example: When learning a new physics formula, relating it to a real-world scenario like projectile motion in sports.
common_misuse: Over-elaborating with irrelevant details that dilute focus on core concepts.

contrast_points: Different from simple repetition because elaboration adds meaningful associations rather than repeated exposure.
evidence_level: High
